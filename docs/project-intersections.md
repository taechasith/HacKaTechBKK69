# Project Intersections: What the HacKaTech Projects Reveal

## Why this page exists

This page reads the HacKaTech 2026 archive as a civic-tech map. It connects early prototypes to public issues, youth learning, technical choices, and event design lessons. It is meant to help future organizers, educators, sponsors, civic-tech researchers, and youth builders understand what the project collection reveals about how young builders approached Bangkok and Thailand's public problems in 2026.

## How to read this analysis

- This is an interpretive archive analysis, not a ranking.
- Projects are treated as early-stage hackathon prototypes, not finished civic products.
- The analysis uses public project links, sanitized project credits, organizer-held participant review and confirmation data, and aggregate participant review themes.
- Private personal data was not published. Names, emails, phone numbers, emergency contacts, payment details, health notes, raw form rows, and private comments are excluded.
- Some civic areas are inferred from project names, public links, or feedback themes. These should be verified by teams before being treated as final project descriptions.
- The optional files `docs/organizer-retrospective.md`, `docs/improvement-roadmap.md`, and `data/feedback_themes.json` were not present in the repository when this page was prepared, so this analysis relies on the sanitized data available in the archive and organizer-provided aggregate findings.

## Overview: The civic map of the archive

Across the archive, projects clustered around daily urban problems: flooding, transport, welfare, public infrastructure, healthcare, public services, climate comfort, sensing, and participation. Participants often translated these civic issues into interfaces: maps, dashboards, websites, sensors, APIs, forms, and demos.

The collection shows young builders treating technology as a civic interface, not just an app. Many projects sit between public data, lived experience, and prototype-making: they ask how people find information, how risk becomes visible, how public services can be understood, and how physical urban systems might be measured or improved.

## Cross-project patterns

### 1. City problems became interface problems

Teams repeatedly translated civic problems into maps, dashboards, websites, sensors, reports, APIs, and demos. Flooding became map-based reporting, risk tracking, flow visualization, or disaster information. Mobility became route planning, city-flow apps, and service navigation. Public infrastructure became sensing, smart poles, hardware prototypes, and public data systems.

This pattern matters because it shows how young builders understood public problems through the tools they could make quickly. A prototype interface became a way to ask: what information is missing, what should be easier to see, and what decision could a user make differently?

### 2. Youth civic tech leaned toward practical pain points

The archive is grounded in practical urban pain points: floods, transport, welfare, public infrastructure, healthcare, public-service access, user safety, and climate comfort. Participant feedback also connected civic work to everyday event realities such as food time, waiting time, crowded rooms, plugs, screens, and audio.

That connection is useful. It suggests that civic-tech learning was not abstract. Participants were learning that public systems depend on details: access, timing, communication, physical setup, safety, and whether people can actually use the service in front of them.

### 3. Open source became public memory

GitHub links and public archive pages matter even when prototypes are incomplete. They preserve the record of what youth teams tried to build, what technical choices they explored, and where the next organizer or mentor can help.

Open source here is not only a software distribution method. It is public memory: a way to keep early civic imagination visible after the event ends, while still respecting participant ownership and privacy.

### 4. Feedback became part of the civic learning process

Aggregate review themes show that judge and mentor feedback helped participants think about evidence, budgeting, feasibility, law, governance, user safety, scope, presentation, data quality, and technical limits. Several projects show teams learning that a strong civic idea also needs a workable implementation path and a clear explanation of who benefits.

This is one of the most important learning outcomes of the archive. The prototypes were not only judged as demos; they became prompts for deeper civic reasoning.

### 5. Event design shaped project quality

Project quality was shaped by event design. Participants valued mentor guidance, friendly staff, hands-on experience, meeting other teams, and seeing real civic problems through different team perspectives. They also identified barriers: unclear timing, late communication, venue crowding, limited plugs, weak audio or screen visibility, judging transparency, long waiting time, short breaks, unclear post-result coordination, online-round readiness, and demo setup risk.

For future organizers, these are not side issues. Communication, venue layout, power access, screen setup, judge flow, mentor access, and demo dry-runs directly affect what teams can build and show.

### 6. Prototype limits are part of the archive

Bugs, local-host failures, LLM instability, hardware problems, unclear project scope, and pitch stress are valuable records of learning. They show where youth teams ran into the real constraints of civic technology: data reliability, public trust, hardware reliability, deployment readiness, and limited time.

The archive should preserve these limits carefully. They are not just failures; they are evidence of what support future civic-tech education needs.

## What the projects collectively suggest about Thai youth civic tech

Participants were not only asking "what can we build?" They were also asking "who is affected?", "what data matters?", "what is feasible?", "what would a public agency actually support?", and "how can people use this?"

The archive shows civic imagination at prototype scale. It includes young people learning software development, website and interface design, GitHub, APIs and map APIs, hardware and sensors, ESP32, boards, Three.js, 3D visualization, AI/LLM limits, project scoping, budgeting, feasibility, user safety, data quality, public rights, welfare, state services, law, governance, pitching, teamwork, time management, and how to turn judge comments into future improvements.

The next step is continuity: stronger documentation, mentorship, public data support, pilot partners, clearer licenses, and post-event incubation. Without that continuity, many prototypes will remain useful memories. With it, some can become better learning tools, public demos, or pilot-ready civic experiments.

## Limitations

- Original private spreadsheets and raw forms are not part of this repository.
- Personal information was excluded.
- Some project civic areas are inferred from names, public links, or feedback themes.
- Some links were duplicated, partial, or uncertain.
- Some projects need better README files, screenshots, demo videos, license information, and team-confirmed summaries.
- This page should be revised if teams submit better descriptions.
- There were 18 review-form project links that did not match the current sanitized archive project list. Examples included deployed pages, Canva links, Google Drive links, Netlify/Vercel/Base44 links, local-host notes, or unclear text. They should not be added as official archive projects unless the organizer later confirms them. This prevents the archive from mixing verified credited projects with unmatched review-only links.
